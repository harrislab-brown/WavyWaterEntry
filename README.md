## Overview
When a hydrophobic sphere crosses an air-water interface it can entrain air, a process crucial in naval, industrial, and environmental settings. While air entrainment through sphere impact onto quiescent baths has been extensively studied, real-world interfaces are inherently wavy, and their influence is less understood. In this Letter, we systematically investigate the effect of surface topology on air entrained by impacting hydrophobic spheres onto an axisymmetric wavefield. By analyzing the air cavity formed behind the impacting sphere across a wide parameter space including wave phase, driving amplitude, and frequency, we reveal that a pre-existing surface topology can dramatically alter air entrainment and air cavity metrics. This effect is driven by topology-dependent splash curtain evolution while air entrainment rate remains constant across all impacts. by shifting the transition criteria between cavity closure modes. The influence of wave properties is well-described by the local wave slope, which alongside the Weber number $We$ and Bond number $Bo$, establishes a complete framework for predicting air entrainment and air cavity metrics across highly dynamic, real-world surfaces like the open ocean.
